An auction is a resource allocation system designed to facilitate the exchange of goods or services through a competitive bidding process. At its core, the system acts as a mechanism to determine the market value of an item by soliciting offers from participants. The primary entities involved include the seller, who provides the item; the bidders, who submit offers; and the auctioneer, who manages the proceedings. While a "forward auction" involves a single seller and multiple buyers competing to offer the highest price, a "reverse auction" involves a single buyer seeking the lowest price from multiple competing suppliers. In some complex "double auctions," both buyers and sellers submit bids simultaneously until supply and demand reach an equilibrium.

The operational workflow of an auction is defined by its price development and disclosure rules. In the common English auction, the process is open and ascending; bidders view the current high bid and must exceed it by a minimum increment to remain competitive. Conversely, a Dutch auction is an open descending process where the auctioneer starts at a high price and lowers it until a bidder accepts. Other systems use "sealed-bid" operations, where participants submit a single private offer without knowledge of rival bids. The winner is typically determined by the highest (or lowest) offer, though the actual payment may vary; for instance, in a Vickrey auction, the winner pays the price of the second-highest bidder to encourage honest valuation.

The system is governed by several critical constraints and edge cases. A "reserve price" serves as a minimum threshold set by the seller; if the final bid fails to meet this amount, the item remains unsold. In contrast, "no-reserve" or "absolute" auctions mandate a sale regardless of the final price. Complexity increases in "multiunit" or "combinatorial" auctions, where bidders may place "all-or-nothing" bids on packages of items rather than individual lots. Finally, time-based constraints, such as the "candle auction" or modern "sniping" protections, regulate the closing conditions to ensure fairness and prevent late-stage manipulation of the bidding window.

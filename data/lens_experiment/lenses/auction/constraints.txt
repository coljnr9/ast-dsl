An auction must involve at least one seller and at least one buyer, but a process with exactly one seller and only one buyer is not considered an auction.
A seller offers goods or services for money or barter exchange, while a buyer pays to acquire them.
In a forward auction, a seller offers items for sale and expects the highest price from buyers.
In a reverse auction, the roles of buyer and seller are reversed to drive the purchase price downward.
A double auction allows for the simultaneous presence of multiple buyers and multiple sellers within the same event.
Under a Walrasian auction, the auctioneer serves as an intermediary who adjusts prices until the supply from sellers exactly matches the demand from buyers.
In an English auction, each subsequent bid submitted by a participant must be higher than the previous bid.
An English auction concludes when no participant is willing to submit a higher bid, at which point the highest bidder pays their bid.
In a Dutch auction, the auctioneer begins with a high asking price and lowers it until a participant accepts the price or the reserve price is met.
In a multi-item Dutch auction, items are allocated in the order bids are received, starting with the first bidder to accept the price.
In a Japanese auction, no participant is permitted to enter or re-enter the bidding process once the auction has commenced.
A Japanese auction terminates immediately when only one participant remains in the process.
In a first-price sealed-bid auction, all bidders must submit their bids simultaneously without knowledge of any other participant's bid.
Bidders in a first-price sealed-bid auction are restricted to submitting only one bid each.
In a Vickrey auction, the winning bidder is the highest bidder, but the price paid is the value of the second-highest bid.
The auctioneer may establish a minimum bidding increment that all subsequent bids must satisfy to be valid.
If a seller sets a reserve price and the final bid fails to reach that amount, the item remains unsold.
In a no-reserve (absolute) auction, the item must be sold to the highest bidder regardless of the final price reached.
Sellers in a reserve auction maintain the right to accept or reject the highest bid if it does not meet their satisfaction.
The reserve price may be kept confidential by the auctioneer or announced publicly to all bidders.
In a combinatorial auction, bidders are allowed to place "all-or-nothing" bids on packages of multiple items.
In a combinatorial auction, the highest individual bidder for a specific item is not guaranteed to win if a combined package bid offers a higher total value to the seller.
A "bid by candle" terminates automatically and unpredictably when the flame of a burning candle expires.
In a Scottish auction, all bidding activity must be completed within a strictly defined time interval.
In a multi-unit uniform price auction, all winning bidders pay the same price for identical items.
In a multi-unit discriminatory price auction, different winning bidders may pay different prices for identical items based on their specific bids.
Auctioneers may accept and execute absentee bids on behalf of bidders according to their prior instructions.
Bidders in an open ascending auction may submit bids vocally, electronically, or through a proxy.
In a barter double auction, participants offer and demand goods based on multiple attributes without the exchange of money.
A seller may withdraw an item from an auction or extend the period indefinitely unless restricted by law or specific terms of sale.

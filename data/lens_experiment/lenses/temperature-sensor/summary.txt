The DS18B20 is a digital thermometer designed to provide high-precision Celsius temperature measurements ranging from -55°C to +125°C. Its primary purpose is to simplify distributed temperature sensing in applications like HVAC controls, industrial process monitoring, and consumer products. The device distinguishes itself by communicating over a 1-Wire bus, a proprietary protocol that requires only a single data line and a ground reference to interface with a central microprocessor. A defining functional characteristic is the "parasite power" mode, which allows the sensor to derive its operating energy directly from the data line, potentially eliminating the need for an external power supply.

The system architecture relies on unique 64-bit serial codes embedded in each device’s ROM, enabling a single master controller to address and manage an almost unlimited number of sensors on a single bus. Internally, the device utilizes a scratchpad memory to store temperature data and configuration settings. A key operation is the temperature conversion, which users can program to resolutions between 9 and 12 bits. This data is stored as a 16-bit sign-extended two’s complement number. Additionally, the system includes nonvolatile alarm registers (TH and TL) that store user-defined upper and lower trigger points. If a measured temperature falls outside these boundaries, the device sets an internal alarm flag, which the master can identify using a specialized search command.

Operational constraints differ based on the chosen power configuration. When using parasite power, the master must provide a "strong pullup" on the bus during temperature conversions or EEPROM writes to ensure sufficient current is available, as the internal capacitor cannot sustain these high-drain operations alone. Furthermore, parasite power is not recommended for environments exceeding +100°C due to increased leakage currents. The 1-Wire protocol also dictates strict timing requirements for initialization and data exchange; if the bus is held low for more than 480 microseconds, all devices on the bus will perform a reset. To ensure data integrity, the system incorporates cyclic redundancy checks (CRC) in both the ROM and the scratchpad memory, allowing the master to validate that transmissions are error-free.

Access control is a security framework designed to regulate how subjects, such as individuals or automated processes, interact with objects like physical rooms or digital resources. The core purpose of the system is to ensure that only authorized entities can enter, use, or consume a resource based on the principle of least privilege, which dictates that users should only possess the minimum access necessary to perform their functions. By managing the "who, where, and when" of access, the system provides a structured environment for maintaining privacy and organizational security.

The system operates through a relationship between credentials, readers, and control panels. A subject presents a credential—categorized as something they know (PINs), something they have (badges), or something they are (biometrics)—to a reader. The reader captures this data and transmits it to a control panel or a central database that evaluates the request against a predefined access control list. If the request is authorized, the system triggers a relay to unlock a physical barrier or grant digital entry while simultaneously recording the transaction in a log for accounting purposes. Modern electronic systems often utilize various network topologies, ranging from centralized hub-and-spoke models to decentralized IP-enabled readers that process decisions at the edge of the network.

To maintain integrity, the system must adhere to specific operational rules and account for various edge cases. Electronic systems are typically configured to monitor the state of a resource, triggering alarms if a door is forced open or held open beyond a set time limit. Important safety constraints include "mechanical free egress," which ensures that individuals can always exit a secure area during emergencies without needing electronic authorization. Security risks often arise from behavioral edge cases such as tailgating, where an unauthorized person follows a legitimate user through a portal. To mitigate these risks and the potential for credential theft, systems frequently implement multi-factor authentication, requiring two or more distinct types of credentials before granting access.

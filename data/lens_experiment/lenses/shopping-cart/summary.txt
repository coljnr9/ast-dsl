Shopping cart software is a specialized e-commerce application designed to facilitate the selection and purchase of products or services over the internet. Its primary purpose is to provide a digital interface where customers can accumulate a shortlist of items for eventual acquisition while allowing merchants to manage their online business operations. The system functions as a bridge between the customer’s browsing experience and the final financial transaction, acting as a real-time repository for selected goods and a calculation engine for the total cost of the order.

The system architecture typically consists of a customer-facing storefront, an administrative backend, and an application programming interface for third-party integrations. Within the storefront, users interact with categories and product listings that are dynamically generated from a central database. When a user selects an item, the system maintains this data using session objects, cookies, or query strings to track the collection as the user continues to browse. During the checkout operation, the software performs critical calculations, including the aggregation of item prices, the application of relevant taxes, and the estimation of shipping, packaging, and postage fees. The administrative component allows merchants to modify the database by adding products, defining discounts, and managing order fulfillment.

The system is governed by strict rules regarding data integrity and security. A primary constraint is the requirement for PCI DSS compliance, which mandates specific standards for handling, transmitting, and storing sensitive payment information to prevent liability. While the software captures payment details, it must often hand off the actual transaction execution to a secure payment gateway rather than processing credit cards directly. Behaviorally, the software must allow users to modify or review their shortlist at any point before the final sale. Once a transaction is finalized, the system must generate a formal order against the selected items and successfully clear the cart data. Furthermore, the system must be capable of tracking abandoned carts—instances where items are selected but the checkout process is not completed—to support subsequent marketing efforts.

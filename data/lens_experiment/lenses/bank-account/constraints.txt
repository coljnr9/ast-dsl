A bank or financial institution maintains financial accounts to record transactions with a customer.
Financial institutions establish the specific terms and conditions for every account type they offer.
A customer is permitted to hold multiple bank accounts simultaneously.
An account is officially created (opened) by the bank in the name of the depositor or a party directed by the depositor.
The acceptance of a customer’s application by a financial institution constitutes a legal contract between the two parties.
Funds deposited into a bank account cease to be the property of the depositor and become the property of the bank.
A depositor holds a legal claim against the bank for the total sum deposited rather than the specific physical currency handed over.
A deposit account is recorded as a liability for the bank and an asset for the account holder.
A loan account is recorded as an asset for the bank and a liability for the borrower.
Funds may only be withdrawn from an account in accordance with its specific terms and conditions.
Transactions occurring within a specific timeframe must be reported to the customer via a bank statement.
The balance of a bank account at any specific point in time represents the customer's financial position with the institution.
Each account must be identified by a unique account numbering scheme managed by the financial institution.
Opening a bank account in a false name is strictly prohibited.
The standard minimum age to open a bank account is 18 years, though some jurisdictions allow it at 16.
Accounts for minors may be opened in the minor's name but must be operated by a parent or guardian.
Banks are permitted to lend some or all deposited money to third parties.
Loan accounts may be secured using collateral provided by the borrower.
A third person may guarantee a loan account, with or without providing additional security.
A credit balance occurs when the financial institution owes money to the customer.
A debit balance occurs when the customer owes money to the financial institution.
Specific account types, such as savings accounts, are categorized by their intended function rather than just the nature of their balance.
Electronic Money Institutions (EMIs) are prohibited from using client funds for lending purposes.
EMIs must safeguard client funds by keeping them separate or depositing them with third-party credit institutions.
EMI accounts typically do not provide the deposit insurance schemes offered by traditional bank accounts.
National laws specify the identification requirements for signatories during the account opening process.
National laws may impose specific limits on the value of deposits and withdrawals allowed for an account.
Certain accounts may transition between credit balances and debit balances depending on the transaction flow.
Traditional bank accounts allow the bank to earn revenue from holding idle deposits, whereas EMIs typically earn from transaction fees.
The system does not support a bailment relationship for deposits; the bank assumes ownership of the physical assets.

A thermostat is a closed-loop control device designed to maintain a physical system at a specific temperature setpoint by sensing thermal changes and triggering corrective actions. It acts as a regulator for heating and cooling equipment, ranging from residential HVAC systems and kitchen appliances to automotive engines and medical incubators. By measuring the error between the desired temperature and the actual measured temperature, the thermostat exerts control by switching devices on or off, or by modulating the flow of heat transfer fluids. These devices are broadly categorized as thermostatically controlled loads and represent a significant portion of global electricity demand.

The system operates through a variety of sensing technologies, including mechanical bimetallic strips that bend with heat, wax pellets that expand and contract, and electronic thermistors that process temperature as electrical signals. In a standard "bang-bang" control model, the thermostat activates the heating or cooling source at full capacity until the setpoint is reached. To prevent the equipment from cycling too rapidly—which causes mechanical wear and inefficiency—thermostats incorporate hysteresis, a functional lag that allows the temperature to oscillate slightly around the setpoint. More advanced systems utilize "anticipators" to shut off the unit just before reaching the target temperature, thereby accounting for the system’s lingering thermal inertia and preventing "overshoot."

Different environments dictate specific hardware configurations and constraints. For example, automotive thermostats use expanding wax to dynamically balance coolant flow, while residential systems often utilize 24-volt low-voltage circuits for safety and compatibility with multiple relays. Digital thermostats offer higher precision through microprocessors and programmable schedules, often employing PID controllers or adaptive algorithms to reach a target temperature by a specific time. A critical constraint in thermostat design is the contact configuration; "normally open" (NO) sets are used for cooling to start a fan when temperatures rise, while "normally closed" (NC) sets are used for heating to initiate a furnace when temperatures drop. Failure to match the thermostat’s voltage capacity to the system—such as using a low-voltage unit on a line-voltage baseboard heater—can result in equipment failure or fire.

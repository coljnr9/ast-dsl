1. A telephone directory must list telephone subscribers within a specific geographical area or service organization.
2. The primary function of the directory is to enable the discovery of a subscriber's telephone number using their name and address.
3. Subscriber names must be listed in alphabetical order within the white pages.
4. Each entry in the white pages must include the subscriber's name, postal or street address, and telephone number.
5. Every subscriber in the coverage area is included in the directory by default.
6. A subscriber may transition to an "unlisted," "ex-directory," or "private" state upon request.
7. Publishers may charge a fee to grant a subscriber's request to remain unlisted.
8. Unlisted subscribers must be excluded from the published directory.
9. Organizations may include instructional content, such as how to use the telephone service or how to dial local and international numbers.
10. The directory must provide access numbers for emergency services, utilities, and medical professionals.
11. Mobile phone numbers and Voice over IP (VoIP) listings are excluded from standard telephone directories in the United States.
12. White pages are designated specifically for personal or primary alphabetical listings.
13. Yellow pages must group business listings alphabetically within specific industry classifications.
14. Business listings in the yellow pages are typically supported by paid advertising.
15. Grey pages (reverse directories) allow a user to find subscriber identity and address details by providing a telephone number.
16. Blue or green pages are reserved for the categorization of government agency information.
17. The directory may change its publication format from hard copy to electronic media or online services.
18. Hard copy directories were traditionally distributed to subscribers free of charge.
19. Electronic directories stored on physical media (CD-ROM) may require multiple discs to facilitate a full reverse lookup.
20. Online directories may integrate phone number lookups with additional features like direct VoIP calling.
21. Modern regulators may waive the requirement for publishers to print residential listings in hard copy.
22. Municipalities are prohibited from banning the unsolicited distribution of phone books in certain jurisdictions despite environmental concerns.
23. Compiling telephone listings does not grant the publisher a copyright over the data in the United States.
24. Historical directory states existed where listings were not alphabetized and lacked individual telephone numbers.
25. The inclusion of supplemental data, such as transit maps, zip code guides, and seating charts, is permitted but not required.

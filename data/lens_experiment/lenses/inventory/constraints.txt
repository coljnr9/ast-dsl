1. An inventory management system tracks inventory levels, orders, sales, and deliveries in real-time.
2. The system can be used to create work orders, bills of materials, and production-related documents for manufacturing.
3. Every inventory item can be tracked via a barcode, serial number, lot number, or revision number.
4. If inventory reaches a defined reorder point threshold, the system triggers a notification to reorder the product.
5. Service-oriented companies can track the cost of materials used during service provision to calculate total service pricing.
6. Product data and orders are inputted into the system using barcode readers, RFID tags, QR codes, or NFC-enabled smartphones.
7. The system uses stock on hand and stock on order data to calculate the required order quantity.
8. The system calculates lead demand to predict the number of units sold during the replenishment lead time.
9. Lifecycle tracking follows products from vendor transport to warehouse storage and finally to retail locations or customers.
10. The system supports the physical movement of goods through receiving, picking, packing, and shipping states.
11. Complex manufacturing processes are managed through multi-level work orders with sequential timelines.
12. The system supports specialized production states through reverse work orders and auto-work orders.
13. Inventory items can be logically grouped or located based on sales popularity or frequently co-purchased associations.
14. System administrators can restrict user rights to grant specific access levels for receiving, ordering, or transferring products.
15. Cloud-based systems allow users to access and update inventory data from any internet-enabled device regardless of location.
16. A single data change in a cloud-based system must replicate company-wide instantaneously and automatically.
17. The system tracks the length of time products are stored to provide insight into stock obsolescence and spoilage.
18. Historical tracking includes the supplier of origin and the specific stocking locations for all products.
19. Cloud-based inventory systems can integrate with external accounting software and online sales channels.
20. Integrated systems sync stock levels across all 3rd party marketplaces and retail channels simultaneously.
21. The system generates reports on inventory accuracy, turnover rates, and profit margins.
22. Total inventory visibility must be maintained across independent departments to allow inter-departmental data retrieval.
23. The system calculates "stock cover" as the remaining days of supply before a projected stockout.
24. Data security prevents unauthorized users from accessing sensitive inventory and financial information.
25. Automated systems are intended to replace manual pen-and-paper or spreadsheet-based tracking methods.
26. Hardware such as barcode scanners or smartphones are required to interface with the digital inventory database.
27. Software updates for cloud-based systems are performed on the provider's schedule rather than the user's schedule.
28. The system must maintain a history of cycle counts to ensure confidence in inventory accuracy.
29. The software facilitates the prevention of both product overstock and out-of-stock situations.
30. The system allows for the tracking of raw materials, parts, and finished goods throughout the manufacturing lifecycle.

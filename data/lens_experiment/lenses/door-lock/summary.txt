A lock and key system is a mechanical or electronic security mechanism designed to control access to a physical space or object. The primary purpose of the system is to fasten a barrier, such as a door or lid, so that it can only be released by an authorized party possessing a specific physical token or secret information. In its most basic form, the system consists of a fastening device and an operating tool. The lock typically utilizes internal obstructions or movable components—such as pins, levers, wafers, or rotating discs—that prevent the bolt from moving. The key acts as the interface, featuring a specific geometry, magnetic orientation, or electronic code that aligns these internal components into a precise state, thereby permitting the mechanism to rotate or slide into an unlocked position.

Operationally, the relationship between these entities is defined by the concept of "keying." In a simple one-to-one relationship, a unique key operates a specific lock; however, more complex configurations allow for "keyed alike" sets or master key systems where multiple distinct keys can operate the same lock. Mechanical pin-tumbler systems, which are among the most common, rely on the physical interaction between the serrated edges of a key blade and a series of spring-loaded pins. Electronic and smart lock variants evolve this relationship by using actuators and electric currents triggered by RFID cards, cryptographic tokens, or biometric data rather than physical torque. In these systems, the authentication happens via a data exchange, but the core objective remains the mechanical retraction of a bolt.

The system is governed by strict rules of physical or logical alignment. For example, in a lever tumbler lock, lifting a lever too far is just as ineffective as not lifting it far enough; both states result in a failure to unlock. Security is constrained by the precision of the manufacturing and the complexity of the internal obstructions, as simpler designs are susceptible to "picking" or the use of skeleton keys. Edge cases include "detector" features that permanently alter the lock’s state if tampering is sensed, notifying the owner of an unauthorized attempt. Additionally, the system must account for physical wear, which is mitigated through locksmithing and key duplication—a subtractive process where a blank is ground to match the specific profile of an original template to ensure continued access.
